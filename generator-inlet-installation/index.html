What "generator inlet installation" actually means

If you own a portable generator, you have two safe and legal ways to connect it to your home: a transfer switch or an inlet box plus a panel interlock. Both stop your generator from feeding electricity back into the utility lines. What you cannot legally or safely do is run an extension cord into a dryer outlet or "suicide cord" the generator into the panel. That back-feeds the grid and can kill a line worker repairing your street.

A generator inlet installation is two pieces working together:

  • The inlet box. A weatherproof outlet mounted on your exterior wall. Your generator's cord plugs into it. From there, the power runs through conduit into your breaker panel.
  • The interlock. A small metal plate inside your panel that slides between the main breaker and a dedicated generator breaker. When the main is on, the generator breaker is physically blocked. When the grid is down and the main is off, you can slide the generator breaker on. It is mechanically impossible to have both on at once. That is exactly what makes it code-compliant, and exactly what the inspector checks.
The honest scope: this powers the circuits you choose, not every circuit at the same time. You decide what matters during an outage, the fridge, the furnace blower, the well pump, a few lights, the internet, and run those through the panel. It is not a standby generator, and it is not a Generac standby system. It is the affordable, legal way to use the generator you already own.

Which circuits can a portable generator actually run?

This is the question sizing guides skip. Your generator's wattage decides how much you can run at once. You add up the running watts of the circuits you want, plus the biggest starting surge (usually the well pump or AC compressor). A rough Upstate SC picture:

  • Refrigerator / freezer: ~600 to 800 running watts (higher surge on start).
  • Furnace blower (gas heat): ~600 to 1,200 watts. This is what keeps the heat on in a winter outage.
  • Well pump (1/2 to 1 HP): ~1,000 to 2,000 running watts, with a large starting surge.
  • Lights + outlets + internet: a few hundred watts total.

A common 7,500-watt portable comfortably covers fridge, furnace blower, well pump, lights, and internet, not all surging at the same instant, which is why you bring loads on one at a time. Running central air conditioning off a portable is usually not realistic, and we will not pretend otherwise. See our full guide on what size generator you need.

FAQ

Common questions

What is a generator inlet installation?

It is a permitted electrical install that adds a weatherproof inlet box to your exterior wall and a mechanical interlock to your breaker panel, so your existing portable generator can power the circuits you choose through the panel. It is not a standby generator and it does not power every circuit at once.

Does a portable generator power my entire home?

No. A portable generator through an inlet and interlock powers selected essential circuits, such as your fridge, furnace blower, well pump, some lights, and internet. You choose which circuits to run based on your generator's wattage. A permanent standby generator is a different, far more expensive product.

Do I need a permit to install a generator inlet box in South Carolina?

Yes. Connecting a generator to your panel is a permitted electrical job, and in SC only a licensed electrical contractor can pull the permit. Backup Power Pro pulls the permit and schedules the county or city inspection. It is included in the price.

Is an interlock kit legal and code-compliant?

Yes. A panel interlock is one of the methods the National Electrical Code accepts for preventing a generator from back-feeding the utility lines. It is mechanically impossible to have the main breaker and the generator breaker on at the same time, which is what the inspector verifies.

Inlet and interlock vs a transfer switch, which is better?

Both are legal and both prevent back-feed. An interlock uses your existing panel breakers, so you can choose any circuit on the panel, and it is usually the lower-cost compliant path. A manual transfer switch pre-wires a fixed set of circuits into a separate box. For most Upstate SC homes pairing an existing portable to essential circuits, we install and recommend the interlock.
