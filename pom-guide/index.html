Your Day-of-Outage Playbook

The exact sequence for your system, start to finish. About 3 minutes once you have done it once.

1

Get your generator to the inlet

Roll or carry your generator to the inlet box at your [INLET BOX LOCATION]. Keep it at least 20 feet from any window, door, or vent, and never run it in a garage or enclosed space.

2

Plug in

Connect your generator cord to the weatherproof inlet box. One cord, one connection.

3

Start it and let it warm up

Start the generator and let it run about 60 seconds with no load before you connect anything.

4

Switch the interlock at your panel

Go to your panel in the [PANEL LOCATION]. Slide the interlock to the generator position. This automatically disconnects you from the grid, which is the safety mechanism that protects the linemen working to restore your power.

5

Turn on your circuits, a few at a time

Flip on the circuits you want. Add them one at a time (start with the fridge and furnace) so the generator handles each motor's startup surge. On your 30-amp system you can run up to about 5,400 watts of steady load comfortably (see your load plan on page 5).

6

When the power returns

Reverse it: flip your circuits off, slide the interlock back to the main position, unplug the cord, then shut the generator down. Done.

Refueling

Only refuel when the generator is OFF and cooled down. Never refuel a running generator. Check your fuel every 4 to 6 hours during a long outage.

Your Load Plan

Your 30-amp connection delivers up to 7,200 watts. Plan steady load at about 5,400 watts (75%) to leave room for motor startups.

A comfortable outage setup

A typical set of essentials you can run at the same time, well within your limit:

CircuitRunning Watts
Refrigerator200 W
Furnace blower700 W
Well pump1,000 W
Sump pump800 W
Lights (3 rooms)150 W
Kitchen + outlets400 W
WiFi + phones + TV150 W
Total steady load3,400 W

That leaves room under your 5,400 watt comfort zone. You can add a microwave or coffee maker for a few minutes at a time, just not while the well pump and sump pump are both cycling. Run the big motor loads one at a time.

With your 50-amp connection (up to 12,000 watts, about 9,000 usable), you have plenty of headroom on top of this. You can add a window A/C unit, an electric water heater on a timer, or run the microwave and coffee maker without juggling the big motor loads.

The one rule

Add circuits one at a time, biggest motors first. With your interlock, every circuit has its own breaker, so managing load is just flipping switches. If the generator bogs down, turn one thing off.

Wattages are common estimates. Your actual appliance draw is on each unit's label. Powers selected circuits through your panel, not the entire home at once.

Your Maintenance Schedule

A generator that will not start is not backup power. Fifteen minutes a quarter keeps yours ready.

Every quarter

  • Run the generator for 15 minutes to keep seals lubricated and the carburetor clear
  • Check oil level and condition
  • Plug into your inlet and flip the interlock to confirm the whole system works before you need it

Before storm season

  • Verify at least 5 gallons of fresh, stabilized fuel on hand
  • Confirm your generator cord is stored with the generator
  • Top off oil, inspect the air filter

Fuel planning

  • Most portable generators burn 0.5 to 1 gallon per hour at half load
  • Keep 10 to 15 gallons on hand for a multi-day outage, in approved containers away from heat
  • Rotate stored fuel every 6 months or treat it with stabilizer

Troubleshooting

If something is not working, start here. Still stuck? Text Key at (864) 863-7800.

The generator runs but nothing in the house has power

The interlock is probably still in the main (grid) position. At your panel, slide the interlock plate so the generator breaker can switch on. The main breaker must be off for the interlock to allow it.

A breaker will not flip on

Confirm the interlock is in the generator position and the main is off. If a single circuit trips when you turn it on, that circuit may be drawing too much, turn off another load first, then try again.

The generator bogs down or stalls under load

You are likely over your comfort zone. Turn off the largest motor load (well pump, sump pump, or A/C), let the generator recover, then add loads back one at a time.

The generator will not start

Check fuel level and the fuel valve, the choke, and the run switch. If it has electric start, the battery may be dead, use the pull start. Old fuel is the most common culprit, see your maintenance schedule on page 6.

The inlet cord will not seat

Line up the cord's twist-lock with the inlet, push in firmly, then twist to lock. It only goes one way. Never force it.

When in doubt

If anything feels off, stop and text us a photo at (864) 863-7800. We installed your system and we know exactly how it is set up.
