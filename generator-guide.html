Backup Power Pro 01, Know Your Generator
Step one
What kind of generator
do you have?
Two things to find out before anything else. Look at your generator right now, the label on the side and the large outlet on the control panel.
Most common
30AMP
5,500 - 8,500 running watts
The most common size. Found at Home Depot, Lowe's, and Costco, most portable generators from major retailers in the last decade are 30A.
Look for a round twist-lock outlet labeled L14-30, usually stamped on the outlet face or on a small plate beside it.
Larger units
50AMP
10,000 - 12,500 running watts
Found on larger generators, typically 10,000 watts and above. More headroom for bigger loads like central AC running alongside everything else.
Look for an outlet labeled L14-50 or 14-50. May be twist-lock or straight-blade, the label is the reliable identifier.
Some generators have both outlets
If yours does, we install one inlet matched to your highest-amperage outlet. So if you have both a 30A and a 50A outlet, we install a single 50A inlet, giving you the most capacity your generator can deliver.
One thing to check before your quote

The inlet requires a 240V outlet on your generator. Find the label on your control panel, don't count prongs, some 240V outlets look different by manufacturer.

L14-30
L14-50
✓ Outlet labeled L14-30 or L14-50 240V, works with the inlet system. Standard on most generators 5,500W and up.
TT-30
L5-30
✗ Outlet labeled TT-30 or L5-30 120V only, not compatible. TT-30 is the RV outlet. L5-30 is a 120V twist-lock. Neither works with the inlet system.

Backup Power Pro 02, Your Power Plan
Things to know
before the lights go out
Read your owner's manual
Your generator's manual lists the exact running watts and surge ratings for your model, the most reliable numbers you'll find. It also covers break-in procedures, oil change intervals, and model-specific warnings that no general guide can cover. If you've lost yours, search your model number + "owner's manual PDF" and the manufacturer's site usually has it free.
Rotate, don't stack
A checkmark means it's a priority, not that everything runs at once. Run the dryer, finish the load, then use the microwave. At any given moment, the total watts of everything running should stay under your generator's running watt rating. Think of it like a budget, you rotate what you spend, not spend it all at once.
Have gas appliances?
Gas water heater, dryer, and stove only use electricity for ignition and controls, roughly 150-400W total for all three. If you have gas appliances, those large loads essentially disappear from your watt total, freeing up most of your generator's capacity for everything else.
Central AC, talk to your HVAC tech first
Running watts are fine on either system. The real challenge is startup surge, an AC compressor can pull 3-6× its running watts for a split second when it kicks on. That spike varies too much between units and generators for us to promise it will work. Your HVAC technician can assess your specific unit and install a soft start kit ($150-300) that reduces the startup surge by 50-70%. That's the right conversation to have before counting on central AC during an outage.

Backup Power Pro 02, Your Power Plan
A few more
things to know
Natural gas generators
Some generators run on natural gas or propane instead of gasoline, no fuel storage, no stale fuel, runs as long as the line has pressure. If you have natural gas service and want to explore this, a licensed plumber can run a gas line to where the generator would sit. It's a separate project from the inlet installation, but the two work together.
Neutral-ground bond
Most portable generators come from the factory with the neutral and ground wires bonded together. That's fine for extension cord use, but when connected to your panel through an inlet, your panel already has its own bond. Two bond points causes GFCI outlets to trip randomly and is a code violation (NEC 250.34). The fix is called a floating neutral, often a removable plug or single wire in the generator's control panel. Search your model + "floating neutral" or ask a small engine shop. We cover this in your installation agreement before any work begins.

Backup Power Pro 03, Safety
Step three
Running it safely
None of what's in this guide matters if the generator isn't run safely. Most of the rules are simple. One of them isn't optional.
Carbon Monoxide, the one that doesn't give second chances
CO is colorless and odorless. It builds up fast and incapacitates before you realize anything is wrong. A single portable generator produces up to 100 times more CO than a car exhaust (National Institute of Standards and Technology). Every year people die during outages not from running generators inside, but from running them just outside a window.
  • Never run a generator inside your home, garage, shed, or enclosed porch. Not with the door open. Not briefly. Never.
  • Keep it at least 20 feet from any door, window, or vent.
  • Point the exhaust away from your home, not sideways along a wall, not toward it.
  • Install working CO detectors on every level of your home. This matters beyond generators.
Running in wet weather
Generators aren't waterproof. A basic generator canopy from any hardware store ($30-50) protects it from rain. Whatever cover you use, make sure airflow around the unit isn't blocked, restricted airflow causes overheating.
Fuel & storage
Always shut the generator off and let it cool 2 minutes before refueling. Store gas in approved containers away from living spaces. If fuel will sit more than 30 days, add a stabilizer, stale fuel is the most common reason generators won't start when needed.
Extension cords
Extension cords run through cracked windows or doors create gaps that let CO drift inside, and they're not a code-compliant way to power your home. If you're in a pinch and must use one outdoors only: 12-gauge or heavier, outdoor-rated, as short as possible. Never coiled, never run under a rug, never through a window or door.
Keep it ready
Run your generator under load for 30 minutes every 90 days, not just start it, actually plug something in and let it work. This keeps the fuel system clean and tells you before a storm whether anything needs attention. A generator that won't start during a storm is worse than not having one.

Backup Power Pro 04, Permits & Protection
Step four
Why the permit matters
to you personally
Some contractors skip the permit. Here's what that decision actually costs the homeowner, not the contractor.
Your insurance
Homeowner's policies include clauses about unpermitted electrical modifications. If an unpermitted installation is connected to any claim, fire, storm damage, anything electrical, your insurer has documented grounds to reduce or deny it. A permitted, inspected installation removes that exposure entirely.
Your home's value
Unpermitted electrical work shows up in buyer inspections. It becomes a negotiating point, a required repair, or a deal-breaker. A permitted, inspected installation is documented as a home improvement that transfers cleanly to the next owner and adds to the sale.
Real safety check
The inspection verifies that the interlock mechanism works correctly, the device that physically prevents your generator and the grid from connecting simultaneously. That protects your family, and it protects utility workers restoring power in your neighborhood who could be harmed by electricity feeding back into lines they believe are dead.
One last thing
The connection that makes
all of this work
Everything in this guide, the wattage planning, the safety rules, the permit protection, assumes your generator is connected to your home properly.
Without that connection, you're back to extension cords through cracked windows. A generator inlet and interlock installation changes that. One cord from your generator to your electrical panel. Flip your priority breakers. Your house runs the way it normally runs.
