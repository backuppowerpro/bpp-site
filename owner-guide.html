Your Backup Power Owner's Guide

Prepared for

Keep this with your generator. When the power goes out, you already know exactly what to do.

When the power goes out

Five steps, about three minutes. It becomes second nature after the first outage.

  1. 1

    Roll it out

    Bring your generator to the inlet box on the side of your house. It runs outside, never in the garage, even with the door open.

  2. 2

    Plug in

    Connect your generator cord to the weatherproof inlet box. One cord, one connection.

  3. 3

    Start it up

    Fire up the generator and let it warm up for about 60 seconds before connecting any load.

  4. 4

    Flip your breakers

    At your panel, switch the interlock to the generator position. Then turn on the circuits you want, one at a time, so starting surges stay manageable.

  5. 5

    When power returns

    Reverse it: breakers off, interlock back to the main position, unplug the cord, shut the generator down. Done.

The three rules that keep everyone safe

Outside only, 20 feet or more from windows, doors, and vents

Generator exhaust carries carbon monoxide, and it can turn deadly in minutes. A garage with the door cracked is not outside. In rain, use a manufacturer-rated canopy, never a loose tarp.

Never plug your generator into a wall outlet

That sends power backward onto the utility lines and can hurt the line crew restoring your power. Your interlock exists so this can never happen by accident: the inlet and your breakers are the one right way to connect.

Refuel only when it's off and cooled down

Never add gas to a running or hot generator. Shut it down, let it cool, then refuel.

Your connection

30-amp inlet

L14-30 · 4-prong twist-lock

Carries up to 7,200 watts at 240V, plenty for a home's essentials: fridge, furnace, well pump, sump pump, and lights. Your generator cord twist-locks into the inlet.

50-amp inlet

CS6365 / 14-50 · 4-prong

Carries up to 12,000 watts at 240V, room to run more at the same time, including larger loads.

The comfortable load rule: plan your steady total at about 75% of your generator's rated output, and add circuits one at a time. With your interlock, every circuit has its own breaker, so you are always in control of what's on.

Before storm season

  • Run it 15 minutes once a quarter. Keeps the seals lubricated and the carburetor clear. About 50 cents of gas.
  • Check the oil level and condition.
  • Keep 5 gallons of fresh fuel on hand, treated with stabilizer if it sits more than 30 days.
  • Test the connection: plug in and flip your interlock once, before you actually need it.
  • Store the cord with the generator so everything is together when it counts.

During a long outage

  • Check fuel every 4 to 6 hours. Most portables burn 0.5 to 1 gallon per hour at half load; a 5-gallon tank runs roughly 6 to 10 hours.
  • Keep 10 to 15 gallons on hand for a multi-day outage, in approved containers, stored away from heat.
  • Shut down and cool before every refuel. No exceptions.
